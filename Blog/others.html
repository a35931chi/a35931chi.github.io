Might Be Relevant

When people ask me about the difference between good and great, I ask them to imagine themselves in an art gallery: Good is something that is aesthetically pleasing. Great is something else that inspires and urges you to express yourself. Your passion boils inside of you and you can't wait to create something that surpass all of your aspirations.

I often get inspired by greatness that surrounds me. When I do, I challenge myself to tell stories through my creations and hoping that my work will also inspire others. I am in love with this endless cycle of being inspired, challenging myself to create, following through on my vision, and inspiring others to think and create.

I am the same way with my profession, personal relationships, family, and hobbies. I am always chasing greatness, and my boiling passion will never go away.

the birth, 20"x20"x25", Plaster and Mixed Media, 2005

Through my travels to Asia and Europe, I witnessed the ubiquitous influence of religion on paintings and sculptures. Most of these works were literal rendering of divine figures. I challenged myself to portray religion figuratively – my creative process had begun.

The ambiguity of this project was how to make my viewers experience divinity without making an apparent reference to religion. I started to brainstorm on images and objects that were often associated with the higher power: the image of sunshine beaming through clouds in the sky, the color white, pyramids, light, etc. While visualizing rays shining through clouds, I began to experiment with the combination of pyramids, the color white, and light.

I made small pyramidal shapes out of white plaster and experimented with stacking them on top of one another. As I built an enclosed cylindrical shape with these plaster units, my sculpture began to take form. Even though each of these plaster units seem lifeless on its own, each piece had a symbiotic relationship with the other. They became a part of the larger object that seemed to take on a serene personality.

I then incorporated a light source within the enclosure. The light penetrated through the crevices and cast shadows onto the sculpture’s surroundings and geometric components. With the introduction of light, the sculpture came to life and inspired an image of the birth of Jesus Christ. To help my viewers make the same associations as I did, I added straw to the base of my sculpture to symbolize a stable.

I wanted to add a vertical element in my apartment because all other architectural elements were compact and low to the ground. A vertical element would visually raise the height of the ceiling. Inspired by African carved wooden giraffes, I made a giraffe using steel wires, glue, and newspaper.

senses, 8"x8"x8", Mixed Media, 2005

People equate what they see with the truth. People are also driven by their curiosity to explore with their sight. In this sculpture, I exploited both of these human qualities.

The checkered pattern and a lock on the box suggested playfulness and secrecy. There was an architectural illusion inside the box. The viewers were encouraged to look through a hole on the box to observe the illusion. After seeing the outside and looking through the hole, viewers would think they know everything there was to know about the content of the box. However, viewers would actually need to unravel the box in order to know the truth. This sculpture conveyed that we are often misled by what we see. If we are unable to unravel the box, then don’t believe in everything we see.
